Use "a, an, the"



The words ‘a’, ‘an’, and ‘the’ are types of articles that are divided into two categories, namely definite and indefinite. The word ‘the’ is definite, which means certain. While the words ‘a’ and ‘an’ are indefinite, which means uncertain or general. We use the word ‘the’ when we want to state something definite. The words ‘a’ and ‘an’ are used when we want to state something that is general.

Using 'a'

To distinguish a and an, what needs to be considered is the first letter of the noun. If the noun begins with a vowel (a, e, i, o, u), then we use “an”, such as “an apple”. While if the noun begins with a consonant, we use “a”, such as “a bicycle”. However, there are exceptions.


  • A Car
  • A Uniform
  • A Book
  • A Brick

Example :

  • A cat passed by in front of my house just now
  • A rabbit is much calmer than cats.
  • I need a book to write.

Using 'An'

The general rule for when to use a or an is that "a" is used before words that begin with a consonant or a vowel that sounds like a consonant, while "an" is used before words that begin with a vowel or an a.


  • An Apple
  • An Umbrella
  • An Iguana
  • An Uncle

Example :

  • They have an old house.
  • She is eating an apple.
  • Jay got an eight score in his IELTS test.

Using The

The word The is often found in English sentences. This word is an article to indicate people or things that have been mentioned, are being discussed, implied or considered familiar.


  • The office (Clarify the Unspoken Words)
  • The ice tea (Explaining Specific Things)
  • The Philippines (Talking About Some Countries)

Example :

  • The raid chair
  • Can I have the candy?
  • he lost the ring.
